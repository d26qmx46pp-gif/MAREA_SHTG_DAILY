## 1. 2026 ACC/AHA/Multisociety Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia

**Title:** *2026 ACC/AHA/AACVPR/ABC/ACPM/ADA/AGS/APhA/ASPC/NLA/PCNA Guideline on the Management of Dyslipidemia*
**Authors:** Blumenthal R, Morris P, Gaudino M, Johnson H, Anderson T, Bittner V, Blankstein R, Brewer L, Cho L, de Ferranti S, Gianos E, Gluckman T, Gradney K, Isiadinso I, Lloyd-Jones D, Marrs J, Martin S, McLain K, Mehta L, Mora S, Mulugeta W, Natarajan P, Navar A, Orringer C, Polonsky T, Reynolds H, Saseen J, Shapiro M, Soffer D, Tynes S, Villavaso C, Virani S, Wilkins J
**Journal:** *Circulation*, April 28, 2026
**PMID:** [41824552](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41824552/) · **DOI:** [10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423](https://doi.org/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001423)

This is the long-anticipated successor to the 2018 cholesterol guideline, and notably the title now reads *Dyslipidemia* rather than *Blood Cholesterol* — reflecting expansion of scope to include hypertriglyceridemia and lipoprotein(a) as first-class entities. The document was developed by a thirty-three-author writing committee co-led by Roger Blumenthal and Pamela Morris and endorsed by eleven societies (ACC, AHA, AACVPR, ABC, ACPM, ADA, AGS, APhA, ASPC, NLA, PCNA). The methods statement notes the literature search was conducted October–December 2024, meaning post-2024 data (e.g., the CORE-TIMI 72a/b olezarsen results published Jan 2026 and the ANGPTL3 program) are not formally evidence-based in this version, though they are likely cited contextually. For our community the key questions to read for are: how the guideline now defines treatment thresholds for triglycerides; whether ApoC-III/ANGPTL-targeted therapies are mentioned as Class IIb/emerging; and how Lp(a) screening is operationalized. Practical impact for SHTG patients is likely modest in this iteration — most evidence-based recommendations will still center on lifestyle, fibrates, omega-3s, and pancreatitis prevention — but the framing matters.

## 2–4. Olezarsen for Hypertriglyceridemia and Pancreatitis Risk — published correspondence

Three letters in NEJM (April 30, 2026 issue, pages 1762–1764) responding to the CORE-TIMI 72a/b trial of olezarsen in severe hypertriglyceridemia (Marston et al., NEJM Jan 29, 2026; PMID 41211918). All share DOI [10.1056/NEJMc2602622](https://doi.org/10.1056/NEJMc2602622).

**Letter 1 — Chen R, Lu S, Chen M (Fuzhou University Affiliated Provincial Hospital).** [PMID 42054688](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42054688/). Reader letter; specific concerns not in PubMed abstract field but likely focused on dose-dependent hepatic fat accumulation and the thrombocytopenia signal at the 80 mg dose, both flagged in the original trial.

**Letter 2 — Wang S, Fan Z (Dalian Medical University–affiliated hospitals).** [PMID 42054689](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42054689/). Second reader letter, similarly structured.

**Reply — Marston NA, Bergmark BA, Sabatine MS (Brigham and Women's Hospital, TIMI Study Group).** [PMID 42054690](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42054690/). Author response from the trial PIs.

The reason this correspondence matters for SHTG/APOC3 work: CORE-TIMI 72a/b was the first adequately powered demonstration that an APOC3-targeted antisense oligonucleotide reduces *clinical* acute pancreatitis (mean rate ratio 0.15, 95% CI 0.05–0.40) on top of large reductions in triglycerides, apoC-III, remnant cholesterol, and non-HDL cholesterol. Open questions that the letters and reply will help frame are (a) whether the dose-dependent rise in hepatic fat fraction with the 80 mg dose is clinically meaningful over multi-year exposure, (b) whether the observed thrombocytopenia signal is mechanism-specific or N-acetylgalactosamine-conjugate–related, and (c) generalizability beyond the predominantly Western trial population — a point the Chinese-affiliated correspondents are well-positioned to raise.

## What to watch

The new ACC/AHA dyslipidemia guideline lands at exactly the moment when the lipid-lowering pipeline is shifting from LDL-only to triglyceride/remnant biology, with olezarsen approved in 2024 for FCS, plozasiran moving forward, and ANGPTL3 programs (evinacumab, ARO-ANG3) maturing. Expect the next 6–12 months of editorials and society commentary to focus on (1) where APOC3- and ANGPTL3-targeted therapies sit in the treatment algorithm for SHTG and pancreatitis risk, (2) whether Lp(a) screening becomes universal under the new guideline, and (3) whether the next iteration of the document — likely 2028 or 2029 — moves from "consider" to formal recommendations once the cardiovascular outcome trials read out. On the bench side, this is also a good week to revisit how genetic LPL-pathway loss-of-function (APOC3, ANGPTL3, ANGPTL4) maps onto pharmacologic mimicry in the trial data we now have.
