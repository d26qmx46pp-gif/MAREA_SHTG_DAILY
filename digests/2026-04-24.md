### 1. Plozasiran Prevents Recurrent Pancreatitis in Adults With Very Severe Hypertriglyceridemia — Post Hoc Analysis of the Phase 3 PALISADE Study

**Authors:** Loomba R et al.  
**Journal:** Alimentary Pharmacology & Therapeutics | May 2026  
**PMID:** [41923352](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41923352/) | **DOI:** [10.1111/apt.70623](https://doi.org/10.1111/apt.70623)

**Summary:** This post-hoc analysis of the Phase 3 PALISADE trial evaluated plozasiran — a quarterly subcutaneous siRNA targeting hepatic APOC3 — in 67 participants with very severe hypertriglyceridemia (median baseline TGs >22.6 mmol/L / >2000 mg/dL) who had at least one prior episode of acute pancreatitis. Plozasiran-treated patients achieved >75% median TG reduction, with median levels falling below 5.65 mmol/L (500 mg/dL). Incident acute pancreatitis occurred in 5/22 placebo-treated participants versus only 2/45 in the pooled plozasiran group — an 83% reduction in risk (HR 0.17; 95% CI 0.03–0.87; p=0.017). Hospitalization rates for abdominal pain and length of stay were also lower with plozasiran. This is the **first therapy to demonstrate a statistically significant reduction in recurrent acute pancreatitis in a prospective, randomized, placebo-controlled trial of FCS patients**, representing a landmark result in the field. The small sample size of the post-hoc cohort warrants caution, but the effect size is striking.

### 2. Olezarsen in Hypertriglyceridemia With High Cardiac Risk: A GRADE-Assessed Meta-Analysis of Randomized Trials

**Authors:** Emara A et al.  
**Journal:** Endocrinology, Diabetes & Metabolism | May 2026  
**PMID:** [42010388](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42010388/) | **DOI:** [10.1002/edm2.70220](https://doi.org/10.1002/edm2.70220)

**Summary:** This meta-analysis pooled 4 RCTs (n=1,615 patients) comparing the APOC3 antisense oligonucleotide olezarsen to placebo in adults with hypertriglyceridemia at high cardiovascular risk. Olezarsen reduced TGs by ~48%, APOC3 by ~69%, VLDL-C by ~49%, and non-HDL-C by ~22%, while increasing HDL-C by ~35%; LDL-C was not significantly changed. Olezarsen was associated with fewer acute pancreatitis events (p=0.035). On the safety side, liver enzyme elevations ≥3× ULN were modestly more frequent with olezarsen (p=0.046). The GRADE-assessed findings confirm robust, consistent lipid-lowering with an overall acceptable safety profile. The absence of a significant LDL-C increase — which distinguishes olezarsen from some other APOC3 inhibitors — is a pharmacologically important observation. Cardiovascular outcomes data remain pending.

### 3. Effect of ANGPTL3 Inhibitors on Lipid Profile and Other Biomarkers: A Meta-Analysis of Randomized Controlled Trials

**Authors:** Xie S et al.  
**Journal:** European Journal of Preventive Cardiology | April 22, 2026  
**PMID:** [42019015](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42019015/) | **DOI:** [10.1093/eurjpc/zwag230](https://doi.org/10.1093/eurjpc/zwag230)

**Summary:** Nine RCTs (n=1,254 participants) comparing ANGPTL3 inhibitors (evinacumab [mAb], vupanorsen [ASO], zodasiran and solbinsiran [siRNAs]) to placebo were pooled. ANGPTL3 inhibition reduced TGs by 47%, LDL-C by 22%, ApoB by 20%, non-HDL-C by 32%, VLDL-C by 41%, and remnant cholesterol by a striking 73%. Circulating ANGPTL3 protein fell by 71%. Subgroup analyses revealed that the mAb evinacumab drove greater LDL-C reductions (relevant for homozygous FH), while siRNAs produced more pronounced VLDL-C lowering — a distinction with mechanistic implications for patient selection. Modest reductions in Lp(a), ApoA1, and ApoE were also noted. No significant effect on hsCRP was observed. The data solidify ANGPTL3 inhibition as a broad-spectrum lipid-modifying approach, but large-scale cardiovascular outcomes trials are still needed.

### 6. Severe Hypertriglyceridemia Due to Lipoprotein Lipase Deficiency in an Infant: Pharmacological and Nutritional Approach

**Authors:** Merino Pardo A et al.  
**Journal:** European Journal of Hospital Pharmacy | April 22, 2026  
**PMID:** [42020202](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42020202/) | **DOI:** [10.1136/ejhpharm-2025-004808](https://doi.org/10.1136/ejhpharm-2025-004808)

**Summary:** A case report describing the management of a 2-month-old infant with genetically confirmed Type I hypertriglyceridemia (LPL deficiency). When conventional dietary fat restriction failed and plasmapheresis was contraindicated, the team turned to intravenous insulin followed by subcutaneous insulin plus omega-3 adjustment, successfully lowering TGs below 1000 mg/dL and achieving clinical stability. While a single-patient report, this case adds practical evidence to an area with almost no controlled data — neonatal and infant SHTG due to biallelic LPL loss-of-function. It also underscores how few approved options exist for LPL-deficient patients, particularly pediatric ones, in the era before gene therapy or APOC3 siRNA are accessible for this age group.
