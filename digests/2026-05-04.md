*New England Journal of Medicine* | 2026 Apr 30 | Vol 394(17):1763–1764

The NEJM this week published two letters and an author reply responding to the CORE-TIMI 72a/72b trials (Marston et al., NEJM Jan 2026, PMID 41211918), which demonstrated that olezarsen — an antisense oligonucleotide targeting APOC3 mRNA — produced 50–72% placebo-adjusted triglyceride reductions and an 85% relative risk reduction in acute pancreatitis among patients with severe hypertriglyceridemia. The correspondents raise questions about generalizability, patient selection, and the mechanism by which APOC3 inhibition translates to reduced pancreatitis beyond its lipid-lowering effects; the Brigham team's reply defends the consistency of benefit across both trial cohorts and emphasizes the magnitude of the pancreatitis signal. The exchange underscores that olezarsen has ignited a genuine scientific debate about whether APOC3 is the dominant driver of pancreatitis risk in SHTG — an important mechanistic question for the field. Full letter texts are not indexed in PubMed abstracts; access requires journal subscription.

For SHTG researchers, this guideline will shape how APOC3 inhibitors, ANGPTL3 inhibitors, and fibrates are positioned in practice and likely influence future drug approval discussions.
