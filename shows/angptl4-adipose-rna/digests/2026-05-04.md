# ANGPTL4 & Adipose RNA — Monday May 4

**Bottom line:** Six new studies reinforce ANGPTL4 as a multi-tissue regulator with relevance to lipid metabolism, diabetic complications, and emerging therapeutic strategies; no adipose-targeted RNA delivery papers appeared in the past 7 days.

---

## Papers

**Unraveling the physiological impact of ANGPTL8 loss-of-function variants in humans**
Abadi A, Chen YQ, Khalid S, et al. *Scientific Reports* (29 Apr 2026) | [PMID 42056369](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42056369/) | [DOI 10.1038/s41598-026-50362-z](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-026-50362-z)

ANGPTL8 coordinates lipid trafficking by forming distinct complexes with ANGPTL3 (which inhibits LPL in oxidative tissues like heart and muscle) and ANGPTL4 (which preserves LPL activity in adipose tissue). Studying protein-truncating variants in a Pakistani cohort, the authors found that ANGPTL8 loss-of-function carriers had lower triglycerides, lower total cholesterol, and higher HDL — and crucially, these variants primarily dismantled the ANGPTL3/8 inhibitory complex while leaving ANGPTL4/8-mediated LPL preservation largely intact. Postprandial triglyceride excursions were also significantly blunted in heterozygous carriers. This elegant dissection of the two complexes helps explain the favorable cardiometabolic phenotype and positions selective ANGPTL3/8 disruption — without perturbing ANGPTL4 function in adipose — as a refined therapeutic target.

**Effects of fenofibrate on angiopoietin-like 3/4/8 proteins and apolipoprotein A5**
Yang Y, Smith S, Chen YQ, et al. *Journal of Lipid Research* (27 Apr 2026) | [PMID 42055284](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42055284/) | [DOI 10.1016/j.jlr.2026.101050](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jlr.2026.101050)

Despite decades of clinical use, the molecular mechanism by which the PPARα agonist fenofibrate lowers plasma triglycerides has not been fully resolved. This study tested whether ANGPTL3, ANGPTL4, ANGPTL8, and APOA5 — key LPL regulators — mediate fenofibrate's TG-lowering effect in both mice and humans. Counterintuitively, fenofibrate elevated ANGPTL3, ANGPTL4/8 complexes, and the pro-atherogenic C-terminal fragment of ANGPTL4, effects that would be predicted to raise TG. These proteins do not appear to be the effectors of fenofibrate's TG reduction, and the elevated C-terminal ANGPTL4 levels are a notable safety concern given their association with increased cardiovascular mortality. The findings urge caution in assuming ANGPTL4 pathway modulation uniformly benefits cardiovascular outcomes.

**ANGPTL4 Induces Aberrant Lymphatic-Like Remodeling in Proliferative Diabetic Retinopathy**
Li Z, Guan L, Mu T, et al. *Diabetes* (01 May 2026) | [PMID 41805865](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41805865/) | [DOI 10.2337/db25-0445](https://doi.org/10.2337/db25-0445)

This paper from the journal *Diabetes* identifies a previously underappreciated mechanism by which ANGPTL4 drives pathology in proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR). The team found that ANGPTL4 and VEGF-C are both elevated in the vitreous of PDR patients who fail anti-VEGF therapy, but ANGPTL4 levels were unaffected by that treatment — suggesting a parallel disease axis. In hypoxic murine retinas, ANGPTL4 (particularly its C-terminal fragment) drove lymphatic-like vessel remodeling via NRP1/VEGFR3 signaling and aberrant immune infiltration; ANGPTL4 knockdown attenuated these effects. ANGPTL4 thus emerges as a therapeutic target for the subset of PDR patients who don't respond to anti-VEGF, addressing both neovascularization and lymphatic dysfunction.

**The PPARβ/Delta-Induced Mesenchymal Stromal Cell Secretome Has Cytoprotective Effects via ANGPTL4 in a Pre-Clinical Model of Acute Lung Inflammation**
Tunstead C, Dunlop M, Ryan S, et al. *FASEB Journal* (30 Apr 2026) | [PMID 42007763](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42007763/) | [DOI 10.1096/fj.202504659R](https://doi.org/10.1096/fj.202504659R)

This study explores how activating the PPARβ/δ nuclear receptor on bone marrow-derived mesenchymal stromal cells (MSCs) enhances their therapeutic secretome in acute respiratory distress syndrome models. The key mediator turned out to be ANGPTL4: PPARβ/δ agonism boosted ANGPTL4 secretion, and this ANGPTL4-high secretome improved lung epithelial barrier integrity, reduced clinical severity scores, and blunted weight loss in LPS-induced acute lung injury mice. Blocking ANGPTL4 with an antibody abolished these benefits, confirming causality. This is a notable proof-of-concept that ANGPTL4 can mediate protective, cytoprotective functions — not just metabolic ones — and that licensing MSCs to upregulate ANGPTL4 is a viable cell therapy augmentation strategy.

**Inhibitory role of angiopoietin-like 4 for cancer progression in oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma**
Mikoshiba T, Sekimizu M, Nakahara N, et al. *Oncology Reports* (Jun 2026) | [PMID 42028751](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42028751/) | [DOI 10.3892/or.2026.9122](https://doi.org/10.3892/or.2026.9122)

In a 137-patient retrospective cohort, high ANGPTL4 expression in oropharyngeal squamous cell carcinoma (OPSCC) tumor tissue was independently associated with improved overall and disease-free survival. When ANGPTL4 was knocked down with siRNA in FaDu head and neck cancer cells, proliferation increased significantly, accompanied by elevated Ki-67. Gene set enrichment analysis pointed to activation of cell-cycle progression pathways. This context-dependent tumor-suppressor role for ANGPTL4 — consistent with some but not all prior cancer literature — argues that any therapeutic strategy aimed at inhibiting systemic ANGPTL4 should account for potential pro-proliferative effects in certain tumor microenvironments.

**Impact of angiopoietin-like 4 (ANGPTL4) protein and O-linked β-N-acetylglucosamine (O-GlcNAc) in diabetic nephropathy**
Pandey A, Badruddeen, Akhtar J, et al. *Diabetes Research and Clinical Practice* (May 2026) | [PMID 41833781](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41833781/) | [DOI 10.1016/j.diabres.2026.113211](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.diabres.2026.113211)

This review synthesizes emerging evidence that ANGPTL4 is a significant driver of diabetic nephropathy (DN) through its roles in LPL inhibition, vascular permeability, oxidative stress, and renal tissue remodeling. The authors also highlight O-GlcNAcylation — a post-translational glucose-sensitive modification upregulated in DN — as a co-regulator of the same signaling networks, creating a potential synergy that accelerates renal injury. Understanding how ANGPTL4 and O-GlcNAc crosstalk may open new combination therapeutic strategies for DN, a disease that remains the leading cause of end-stage renal disease globally.

## What to Watch

The most clinically actionable thread this week is the tug-of-war between the two ANGPTL4 fragment activities: the N-terminal fragment that inhibits LPL (pro-metabolic when targeted) versus the C-terminal fragment that drives angiogenesis, vascular permeability, and — as the fenofibrate paper warns — may increase cardiovascular risk when elevated. The ANGPTL8 variant study elegantly shows that selectively dismantling ANGPTL3/8 complexes without disrupting ANGPTL4/8's protective role in adipose is biochemically achievable. Watch for therapeutic programs that target this specificity. On the disease-complication front, the diabetic retinopathy paper from *Diabetes* is particularly compelling: ANGPTL4 is elevated in the vitreous of patients who fail anti-VEGF, suggesting a new combination target for refractory PDR. No adipose-targeted RNA delivery papers appeared this week — a gap that makes the ANGPTL8/ANGPTL4 mechanistic work all the more relevant as a potential future RNA delivery target definition.
