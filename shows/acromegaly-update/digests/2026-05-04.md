# Acromegaly Update — Monday May 4

**Bottom line:** Two important consensus-level papers from late April 2026 sharpen the standards for acromegaly diagnosis and comorbidity management, incorporating AI-assisted screening, ultrasensitive GH assays, and updated international expert guidance.

---

## Papers This Week

### 1. **Diagnosis of Acromegaly**
**Authors:** Yogi-Morren D, Chanson P  
**Journal:** *Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism* | Published: April 29, 2026  
**PMID:** [42056759](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42056759/)  
**DOI:** [10.1210/clinem/dgag189](https://doi.org/10.1210/clinem/dgag189)

This review from Cleveland Clinic and Université Paris-Saclay offers a comprehensive update on the diagnostic landscape of acromegaly, a disease still plagued by an average diagnostic delay of 6–10 years. The authors highlight that while classical features like acral enlargement and facial coarsening remain diagnostically significant, many patients first present with nonspecific symptoms — sleep apnea, carpal tunnel syndrome, arthralgias, and metabolic disturbances — making early recognition particularly challenging. A major theme is the integration of artificial intelligence: comorbidity cluster analysis and AI-assisted facial recognition tools are now being studied as potentially powerful screening adjuncts, especially in patients without overt acral changes. On the biochemical front, the review underscores advances in assay harmonization and age-adjusted reference ranges for IGF-I, and notes that ultrasensitive GH assays have lowered the recommended nadir GH cut-off during oral glucose tolerance testing to approximately 0.4 µg/L — with important assay-specific caveats. The authors also review the expanding role of high-resolution MRI, PET/MRI, and radiomics for earlier and more accurate tumor localization.

### 2. **Consensus on Acromegaly Complications: An Update**
**Authors:** Giustina A, di Filippo L, Fleseriu M, Pivonello R, Petersenn S, Wass J, Samson SL, and 36 additional international experts  
**Journal:** *Pituitary* | Published: April 29, 2026 (Vol. 29, Issue 3)  
**PMID:** [42050227](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42050227/)  
**DOI:** [10.1007/s11102-026-01639-z](https://doi.org/10.1007/s11102-026-01639-z)

The 16th Acromegaly Consensus Conference (held in September 2024) convened 43 international experts to update recommendations for the management of acromegaly comorbidities, building on the 2020 guideline. The updated consensus reflects new evidence on both established and emerging comorbidities, as well as evolving pharmacological strategies — with particular attention to the impact of current and investigational therapies including pasireotide, pegvisomant, and newer oral somatostatin receptor ligands. The expert panel reviewed literature across cardiovascular, metabolic, skeletal, and oncologic domains, reassessing outcome goals in the context of newer treatments. The paper provides actionable guidance on drug selection for specific comorbidity profiles, representing a practical decision-making resource for endocrinologists managing complex acromegaly patients. This consensus update is especially timely given the growing pipeline of GH-axis–targeting agents approaching or recently reaching the market.

## What to Watch

This week's papers together form a cohesive diagnostic-to-management picture of acromegaly in 2026. On one side, AI-driven tools and ultrasensitive biochemical thresholds are pushing the boundaries of earlier detection — a critical advance given that structural and metabolic damage accumulates silently during the multi-year diagnostic gap. On the other, the 16th Consensus Conference signals maturing guidance on how to manage the full comorbidity burden once diagnosis is established. The near-term frontier will likely be the intersection of these two threads: identifying patients earlier while simultaneously deploying next-generation agents that more precisely target GH/IGF-I excess without the glycemic liabilities of first-generation somatostatin analogs. Paltusotine, the oral somatostatin receptor ligand, remains a key drug to watch as further real-world data accumulate.
